1. The Austin Chronicle bills the display at the Goodwill Computer store as a museum. Consider you own experiences visiting museums, both as child and as an adult. Why did or do you specifically, and people in general, visit museums? What activities define your conception of a museum going experience? What should a museum look like and offer? I visited museums as a child just for entertainment value with my family. My parents enjoyed bringing my siblings and myself there because we had fun while learning a bit more about the world we lived in. Museum-going, to me at that age, was merely a family entertainment-venture and nothing more.
Now, although I still visit museums for entertainment and often with family (although no longer limited to), I am much more interested in the facts and information the museums have on their specific genre. Rather than merely looking at the “neat looking fish” when I was a child, I now read what the museum has about that fish.
I believe that people in generally visit museums for two reasons (usually both combined); for fun/amusement and to learn. Often when you become fascinated with something (which museums can encourage), you will enjoy learning about it. That’s what museums do.
Museums, I believe, should not be strictly restricted to what they should look like, but rather that they offer a wealth of information and convey it in an interesting manner. A high-tech museum may have holograms of certain animals while another may have statues or even stuffed deceased animals. The way the exhibits look should not really matter, as long as information can be adequately learned from them. The Goodwill Museum, I believe, is actually a museum. Its exhibits are old computers and people may enjoy reading about and seeing decade old (or older) computers such as Crays, IBMs, etc.

6. Outline the Long Now Foundation's recipe for preservation. Be specific in describing what they deem necessary. Does the Goodwill Computer Museum and Store, as described in the Austin Chronicle, run along these lines? What about the AtariAge gaming community or those involved in video game emulation?

The Long Now Foundation believes that merely having a digital copy of something is inadequate. They quote statistics such as magnetic media only lasting “5 to 10 years” and CD-ROMs “if used only once, last only 5 to 15 years.” They think that exercise is the best way to preserve something. One of their proposals was to create artificial intelligence that would continuously use, exercise, and copy the media to extend its life. In order for something to be preserved, it must be constantly used and translated/migrated to new platforms while leaving a chain recording the translation/migration so that it would be possible to back-track and begin again.
I don’t think the Goodwill Computer Museum runs along those lines, primarily, because the items are not put to use. A major difference also is that the Goodwill Museum records history by preserving the actual item, rather than digital forms of it. Therefore, you cannot translate and/or migrate to new platforms. The exhibits in the museum lay dormant and are not exercised at all. There is no one continuously using the computers and some of the computers (such as the Cray) cannot be operated anyways because of the lack of software.
The AtariAge gaming community and video game emulation more closely runs along the lines of the Long Now Foundation because they are continuously using Atari software and translating/updating/migrating it to new platforms (emulators). They are preserving the technology by continuing to use it, the best way according to the Foundation.

The ideas that the Goodwill Computer Museum represents, I think, will continue to live on. Similar to when comic books were first released, there were no museum exhibits for them or large-scale collectors, but as time went by, their value and worth grew and grew and now some of the old ones are worth lots of money and are showcased by collectors and/or museums.
The ideas will continue to live on, perhaps not through the Goodwill Museum but by other, similar projects. The Austin Chronicle states that the Smithsonian has a computer history collection and that there are a few (although not many) others across the country. Since the Smithsonian is such a large prominent museum, I don’t think that the ideas of preserving computer technology will die anytime soon. There are also the works and collections of private collectors who will make the idea live on.
In one or ten years there may not be much of a difference in respect to computer history, but I think that after 20 to 25 years, there will be more and more organizations and project that will attempt to preserve old computer technology. Computer history will probably be similar to car/automobile history. People will continue to use the new cars/computers, some will use the old, but we will preserve the old ones to record information about objects/technology that greatly impacted human society.

9. Is there anything particular about computer and digital technology that makes its preservation different from other forms of technology? Does the fact that the Museum portion of the Goodwill Computer Store is no longer open to the public relate to possible differences in preservation?

Computer and digital technology is different from other forms of technology in regards to preservation because a lot of the technology cannot be viewed directly and can be easily lost (which is what the Long Now Foundation said). Often, when a small portion of digital technology is corrupted, the whole thing is corrupted and not viewable. For other types of technology, if something is broken, you may still be able to examine parts of it.
Preserving the actual hardware (in non-working condition) does not differ too much from other technologies (such as old telephones, typewriters, etc.). Therefore, I do not believe that the fact that the Museum is no longer open to the public relates to the difference of preservation. The Museum basically showcased the physical hardware components of computers, not the software (the computers were not running with software viewable).
I think that the Museum was basically shut-down due to insufficient man-power/demand/interest. And perhaps some rude people really were stealing from it (tying into not enough man-power to watch over the collection). I don’t think it had a lot to do with the differences in preservation.
