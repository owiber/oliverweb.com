About the Orchestra

McNeil's Sinfonietta and Symphonic orchestras, both under the conduction of Mr. Allan McKin, have a combined membership of thirty nine. They are string orchestras , but we do play as a full orchestra with a few wind players from the band once a year.

The four instruments of a string orchestra are the violin, the viola, the violincello, and the doublebass. The violin is the instrument with the highest register. It is sometimes called a fiddle. It has a bright sound which suits the melodic lines it often carries.

The next instrument of the orchestra is the viola. It has a slightly lower range of notes, and a much richer, deeper sound. Violists are often the accompanists of the orchestra. A viola is shaped like a violin, although it is generally bigger in thickness and length. The dimensions are arranged in such a way that it is considered more difficult to play than a violin -- one reason why there is not as much music composed for the viola. Sad, indeed, that such an exquisite instrument should be so neglected. :~~(

Next is the cello. Seen Yo-Yo Ma on Letterman? Well, that's a cello. It is lower in range than both violin and viola (cellists read bass and tenor clefs). The cello is very versatile in it's possible sounds. Cello parts are written for melody about as often as they are written for harmony. [I just found a cute trick I thought I'd share with you all: if you open a .wav of a violin at about 6kHz lower than it was recorded, it sounds exactly like a cello. :) ]

Finally, the bass is the lowest and largest instrument in the orchestra. It is required to stand or sit on a stool to play this monster. As far as range, the closest I could relate it to would be a tuba -- when they hit their lowest note, you feel it. They often play notes that are the basis for chord structures of the music.
